The United Nations

The United Nations, currently composed of 193 Member States and two non- member observer states (the Holy See and the State of Palestine), is the largest and most powerful intergovernmental organization in the world with its main offices located in New York, Geneva, Nairobi, Vienna and The Hague. The UN is not a supra-State, but, an independent organization that does not have an army and imposes no taxes. It came into existence on 24 October 1945 after the ratification of the United Nations Charter which includes rights, obligations, purposes and principles of the Organization and its Member States. The international character of the Charter allows the United Nations to take action on issues such as peace and security, climate change, human rights, disarmament, terrorism, governance, food and more. The other documents that inspire and regulate the mission of the Organization are the Universal Declaration of Human Rights which was proclaimed as a common standard of achievements for all peoples and all nations, the Convention on the Rights of the Child, and the Statute of the International Court of Justice.

Leadership

Symbol and spokesman of the United Nations ideals is the Secretary-General, at the moment, Mr. António Guterres who took office on 1 January 2017. The Secretary-General is described by the UN Charter as the “chief administrative officer” of the Organization. He is invested by the responsibility of bringing in front of the Security Council any matter that could threaten international peace and security, even at the risk of challenging or disagreeing with UN Member States. The Secretary-General is also the chief administrative of the UN Secretariat and Chairman of the United Nations System Chief Executive Board for Coordination (CEB) which brings together the Executive Heads of all UN programmes, funds, and specialized agencies. The CEB is the highest-level coordination forum of the United Nations. Its main responsibility is to provide coordination for strategic guidance, coherent leadership, shared vision on areas under the authority of Executive Heads. The work of CEB is supported by two high-level committees: The High-level Committee on Programmes and the High-level Committee on Management. Under the committees there are inter-agency networks to coordinate specific topics.

Main Organs and general structure

The United Nations is constituted of six main organs. Five of them, the General Assembly, the Secretariat, the Economic and Social Council, the Security Council, the Trusteeship Council are headquartered in New York, while the International Court of Justice is based in The Hague. The General Assembly is the deliberative and policymaker organ of the UN where all 193 Member States are represented. According to the UN Charter, the Assembly may elect non-permanent members of the Security-Council, initiate studies and make recommendations to promote political and economic cooperation and realization of human rights. Decisions on questions such as peace and security, admission of new members and budgetary matters required the two-third majority of the Assembly, while, decisions on other issues are simply by majority. During the Assembly’s annual general debate, which provides Member States the opportunity to express their view on specific issues, the Assembly allocates to its six Main Committees items relevant to their area of competence. The Secretariat comprises the Secretary-General, currently, Antonio Guterres, and the UN international staff which carries out the day-to-day duties of the UN. It is organized along departmental lines with each department having a distinct area of action. The Economic and Social Council is the organ for coordination, policy dialogue and recommendation on economic and social matters. It also serves as the central mechanism for the activities of the UN system and its specialized agencies, and to supervise subsidiary and expert bodies. The security Council has responsibility for the maintenance of peace and security, it has fifteen members of which five (USA, France, UK, China and Russia) are permanent. The Trusteeship Council has been established to provide supervision over 11 Trust Territories. Today, these territories are independent, thus, the Council is inactive. Lastly, the International Court of Justice is the principal judicial organ of the UN with the aim of settling legal disputes or giving advisory opinions on legal issues in accordance with international law.

In the UN system, below the six main organs, sit a variety of organizational entities with different institutional and functional structures. There are funds, programmes and offices that report annually to the General Assembly or the Economic and Social Council; specialized agencies that have their own legislative and executive bodies and report to the ECOSOC; and related agencies that are connected to the UN through cooperative agreements. Non-governmental organizations may also participate in the UN work if granted with consultative status with ECOSOC and other agencies.

There are also Regional Commissions which are under the authority of ECOSOC. They assist in the economic and social development of their respective geographic areas and strengthen economic relations among the countries in each region. They consist of the Economic Commission for Africa, the Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific, the Economic Commission for Europe, the Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean, and the Economic and Social Commission for Western Asia.

Budget

All the UN’s agencies, funds, programmes, offices and commissions have their own independent leadership and budget. There are three types of funding within the UN. Regular budget, the peacekeeping budget and a “voluntary funding”. The regular budget is compulsory for all nations even though it differ widely from state to state. Peacekeeping payments are not required from the poorest nations, and, to make up for this, the five permanent members of the Security Council make extra payments. Voluntary donations are generally used to finance funds and programmes, instead, specialized agencies are autonomous organizations financed through both assessed and voluntary contributions. If nations become two years late with compulsory payments, they lose their right to vote in the General Assembly.

Cooperation with global civil society groups

Non-governmental organizations participate vitally within the United Nations. They contribute with information, advocacy and essential operational capacities in emergencies and development efforts. The Secretary-General has reaffirmed several times the importance of NGOs describing them as “indispensable partners” to the UN in deliberating and executing policies. Currently, there are 5451 NGOs enjoying the consultative status with ECOSOC which permits to access not only the Economic and Social Council, but, also its subsidiary bodies, the different human rights mechanism of the UN, ad-hoc processes on small arms and events organized by the President of the General Assembly. Furthermore, NGOs may participate to the Civil Society Unit within the Outreach Division in the United Nations Department of Global Communications (DGC). Over 1500 civil society organizations are in formal association with the DGC gaining access and helping disseminating information about issues of concern to the UN.

How the United Nations implements its principles

The UN expresses its recommendations and rulings through documents called resolutions. In theory, any UN body can issue a resolution, in practice, only three adopt resolutions, namely, the General Assembly (GA), the Security Council (UNSC) and the Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC). It is the issuing body that determines if a resolution is binding or not, in general, those issued by the GA and the ECOSOC are considered as recommendations, while, those issued by the Security Council are binding. In reality, the enforcement of resolutions is less straightforward and it depends on a number of factors.

The Security Council can also take actions to maintain or restore international peace and security through economic and trade sanctions, travel bans, arms embargoes. Since the 1966, the Council has applied 30 sanctions regimes. Today there are 14 ongoing sanctions regimes aimed at supporting political settlement of conflicts, nuclear non-proliferation, and counter-terrorism. Each regime is administered by a sanction committee chaired by a non- permanent member of the UNSC.

In general, the work of the UN system is divided into five main areas of interest around which are created the necessary bodies. For example, the first area of action is international peace and security. It includes preventive diplomacy and mediation that are implemented through special envoys and missions on the field; peacekeeping which gives life to peacekeeping operations; peacebuilding with peacebuilding offices, commissions and funds; counter-terrorism which includes different programmes such as chemical biological and cyber counter terrorism; disarmament supported by the Office for Disarmament Affairs. The second area of action is protection of human rights which is mostly enabled by the High Commissioner for Human Rights, the Human Rights Council, the General Assembly’s Third Committee, several Special Representative of the Secretary-General, the Commission of the Status of Women, and UN Women. The third topic is humanitarian aid, managed by the Office for Coordination on Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) and delivered by several entities among which there are the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), the United Nations Refugee Agency (UNHCR), the United Nations Children’s Fund (UNICEF), and the World Food Programme (WFP). The fourth is sustainable development supported by offices and programmes related to natural resources and disaster risk reduction. Lastly, international law which is promoted principally by the International Court of Justice (ICJ) plus a wide variety of international courts.
